\begin{abstract}
Hippocampal volume is a key biomarker for Alzheimer's disease and brain
aging, yet reported sex differences vary substantially depending on
head-size adjustment methods. We examined whether the
hippocampal-to-ventricle ratio (HVR), a self-normalizing measure,
provides more consistent sex difference estimates than conventional
adjustment approaches. Using a subset of UK Biobank structural MRI data
(N = 27,680; 56\% female; ages 45--82), we compared sex differences
across four adjustment methods (unadjusted, proportions, stereotaxic,
residualized) and in samples matched on age and on intracranial volume
(ICV). Hippocampal sex differences reversed direction across methods,
ranging from \(d = -0.89\) (males larger, unadjusted) to \(d = 0.58\)
(females larger, proportions), with a range of 1.5 standard deviations
across analytical choices. In contrast, HVR showed a consistent female
advantage (\(d = 0.52\)) that persisted in ICV-matched samples
(\(d = 0.19\)), confirming this effect is not a head-size artifact.
Males exhibited steeper cross-sectional age-related HVR decline
(\(1.7\times\) female rate, \(p < 10^{-50}\)), consistent with
earlier-onset male ventricular expansion. Structural equation modeling
revealed that HVR predicted general cognitive ability comparably to
hippocampal volume (\(\beta = 0.04\), overlapping CIs), and unlike
residualized hippocampal volume, HVR maintained significant
brain-cognition associations in both sexes. We provide sex-specific
normative centile curves for clinical application. These findings
indicate that apparent hippocampal sex differences largely reflect
methodological choices rather than biology, while HVR captures
consistent morphological variation related to brain aging that may have
clinical utility.
\end{abstract}


\section{Introduction}\label{introduction}

The hippocampus (HC) is a brain structure used as an early biomarker for
Alzheimer's disease (AD) and brain aging {[}Jack et al., 1999; Jack et
al., 2010; Raz et al., 2005{]}. Sex differences in brain structures have
been documented, but their interpretation is controversial {[}Ritchie et
al., 2018; Ruigrok et al., 2014{]}, largely because findings depend on
whether and how head-size adjustments are applied {[}Nordenskjöld et
al., 2015; Sanchis-Segura et al., 2020{]}. Males have approximately 12\%
larger intracranial volume (ICV) than females {[}Ruigrok et al.,
2014{]}, and after head-size correction, sex classification from brain
features drops from \textgreater80\% to approximately 60\%
{[}Sanchis-Segura et al., 2020{]}. This methodological sensitivity
suggests some reported sex differences may be artifacts of adjustment
methods.

Hippocampal atrophy rates predict conversion from mild cognitive
impairment to dementia {[}Rajagopal et al., 2024{]}, making accurate
volume assessment clinically consequential. However, males and females
show different rates of age-related brain volume change, with steeper
subcortical volume declines in males across multiple structures
{[}Ritchie et al., 2018{]}. These sex-differential trajectories mean
that normative reference values must be sex-stratified to avoid
misclassifying normal variation as pathological; recent large-scale
brain charting efforts have adopted sex-specific centile modeling for
this reason {[}Bethlehem et al., 2022; Ge et al., 2024{]}.

Multiple adjustment methods exist, each with different assumptions:
proportions (V/ICV) assumes perfect allometric scaling, residualization
(V \(-\) \(\beta \times\) ICV) removes linear ICV association, and
stereotaxic normalization applies spatial transformation to a template.
These methods can yield contradictory conclusions about the same brain
regions {[}Wang et al., 2024{]}. For hippocampus, reported sex
differences range from males larger (unadjusted; Ruigrok et al.
{[}2014{]}) to females larger (proportions; Sanchis-Segura et al.
{[}2020{]}) to no difference (residualized; O'Brien et al. {[}2011{]}).
The proportions method can produce biologically implausible results,
while residualization yields more consistent estimates {[}Wang et al.,
2024{]}. No consensus exists on the correct method, and conclusions
depend on analytical choices.

The hippocampal-to-ventricle ratio (HVR), defined as HC / (HC + LV)
where HC denotes total hippocampal volume and LV denotes the volume of
the temporal horns of the lateral ventricles, offers a potential
solution. Both structures scale with head size, making their ratio
self-normalizing. HVR captures the relationship between hippocampal and
ventricular volumes, which show opposite patterns with age---smaller
hippocampus and larger ventricles. Schoemaker and colleagues introduced
HVR and showed that it provides stronger associations with age and
memory than standard hippocampal volume in healthy aging {[}Schoemaker
et al., 2019{]}. Subsequent work shows HVR provides higher effect sizes
for distinguishing AD from controls compared to HC alone
{[}Fernandez-Lozano et al., 2025{]}. However, properties of HVR in
healthy populations and across adjustment methods remain unexplored.

Age-related ventricular expansion reflects concurrent loss of
surrounding gray and white matter tissue {[}Madsen et al., 2015{]}, and
this expansion occurs earlier in males, with abnormal enlargement
appearing in the seventies and preferentially affecting the frontal
horns {[}Currà et al., 2019{]}. These sex-differential ventricular
trajectories suggest that a ratio incorporating both hippocampal and
ventricular volumes may capture sex differences in brain aging that
individual volumes miss. Furthermore, brain structure--cognition
associations themselves may differ by sex: grey matter volumes predict
cognitive profiles with different strength and spatial specificity in
older males versus females {[}Jockwitz et al., 2024{]}, and hippocampal
volume predicts associative memory in older women but not men {[}Zheng
et al., 2017{]}. Whether HVR shows similar sex-differential cognitive
associations remains unknown.

We addressed three questions: (1) Do hippocampal sex differences vary
across head-size adjustment methods while HVR remains consistent? (2)
Does HVR show comparable cognitive associations to conventionally
adjusted hippocampal volumes? (3) Do cross-sectional age patterns differ
by sex, and do these patterns differ between HC and HVR depending on
normalization method?

\section{Materials and Methods}\label{materials-and-methods}

\subsection{Data Source and
Participants}\label{data-source-and-participants}

Data were obtained from UK Biobank (Application 45551) {[}Miller et al.,
2016; Sudlow et al., 2015{]}. The initial imaging sample comprised
approximately 47,398 participants with T1-weighted structural MRI. After
exclusions (detailed below), the final analytic sample included N =
27,680 participants aged 44.6 to 82.8 years. With this sample size, we
had \textgreater99\% power to detect small effects (d = 0.10) and
\textgreater80\% power to detect very small effects (d = 0.05) for
two-group comparisons at \(\alpha\) = .05.

Three additional subsamples were defined for specific analyses
(Figure~\ref{fig-prisma}; Table~\ref{tbl-demographics}). The
\textbf{England subsample} (N = 25,172) comprised participants assessed
at UK Biobank centres in England and was used for SEM analyses requiring
the Index of Multiple Deprivation (IMDP), which is not comparable across
UK countries. This subsample provides adequate power for detecting
standardized path coefficients \(\geq\) 0.02. The \textbf{ICV-matched
subsample} (N = 9,500) comprised female--male pairs matched on age
(\(\pm 1\) year) and ICV (\(\pm 25\) cc) drawn from the primary sample,
and was used to isolate sex effects from head-size confounding (Analysis
2). The \textbf{longitudinal subsample} (N = 2,935) comprised
participants from the primary sample who also had a follow-up imaging
session (ses-3), with a mean intersession interval of 2.7 years (SD =
1.1; range: 1.5--5.0). This subsample was used as a sensitivity check
for normative model calibration (Analysis 4).

\subsubsection{Exclusion Criteria}\label{exclusion-criteria}

Participants were excluded if they had any of the following ICD-10
diagnoses recorded in linked hospital records: F00-F99 (mental and
behavioral disorders including dementia, mood disorders, schizophrenia,
and anxiety disorders, which are associated with hippocampal volume
alterations {[}Brosch et al., 2022; Cao et al., 2023; Schmaal et al.,
2016{]}); G00-G99 (neurological diseases including Parkinson's disease,
multiple sclerosis, epilepsy, and cerebrovascular disease, which
directly affect brain structure {[}Erp et al., 2016; Koenig et al.,
2014; Tai et al., 2025; Xu et al., 2020{]}); and Q00-Q07 (congenital CNS
malformations affecting normal brain development). This exclusion
strategy selected a sample of cognitively healthy adults without
conditions known to affect hippocampal morphometry. The full exclusion
flow is shown in Figure~\ref{fig-prisma}.

\subsubsection{Quality Control}\label{quality-control}

Image segmentations underwent quality control in three stages: (1)
automated quality scores from deep learning-based assessments, requiring
DARQ score \(\geq\) 0.25 {[}Fonov et al., 2022{]} and AssemblyNet rating
of ``A'' (highest quality grade) {[}Coupé et al., 2020{]}, (2)
statistical outlier detection excluding participants with age- and
head-size-adjusted volumes exceeding \(\pm 3\) SD from the sample mean,
and (3) visual inspection of flagged cases by trained raters for evident
segmentation failures, motion artifacts, or anatomical anomalies.

For participants with multiple imaging sessions, QC filtering was
applied first, then one session per participant was selected for
cross-sectional analyses. When a participant's baseline session (ses-2)
failed QC but the follow-up session (ses-3) passed, the follow-up
session was retained in the cross-sectional sample. Longitudinal
analyses required both sessions to pass QC.

\subsection{Brain Measures}\label{brain-measures}

\subsubsection{Image Acquisition}\label{image-acquisition}

Images were acquired on Siemens Skyra 3T scanners at three UK sites
(Cheadle, Newcastle, Reading) using a T1-weighted MPRAGE sequence at 1mm
isotropic resolution. Full protocol details are provided in
Alfaro-Almagro et al. {[}2018{]}.

\subsubsection{Segmentation}\label{segmentation}

Hippocampal and ventricular volumes were segmented using an in-house
convolutional neural network trained on manually labeled data.
Validation against manual segmentations showed Dice \(\kappa\) = 0.94
and Pearson r = 0.99 {[}Fernandez-Lozano et al., 2025{]}. Outputs
included bilateral hippocampal volumes (left, right, total) and lateral
ventricular volumes (left, right, total). Intracranial volume (ICV) was
obtained from AssemblyNet {[}Coupé et al., 2020{]}, a large-ensemble
convolutional neural network for whole-brain MRI segmentation.

\subsubsection{Head-Size Adjustment
Methods}\label{head-size-adjustment-methods}

Four adjustment approaches were applied to hippocampal and ventricular
volumes {[}Sanchis-Segura et al., 2020; Sanfilipo et al., 2004{]}:

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}
\item
  \textbf{Unadjusted}: raw volumes in native space (mL), which preserve
  natural variation but are confounded by overall head size.
\item
  \textbf{Proportions}: volume expressed as a proportion of ICV, which
  assumes isometric (1:1) scaling and may over- or under-correct
  depending on the true allometric relationship {[}Jong et al., 2017;
  Williams et al., 2021{]}:
\end{enumerate}

\begin{equation}\phantomsection\label{eq-prp}{V_{\text{PRP}} = \frac{V}{\text{ICV}} \times 1000}\end{equation}

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}
\setcounter{enumi}{2}
\tightlist
\item
  \textbf{Stereotaxic}: volumes in standard template space after
  nonlinear registration, which corrects for global size and shape
  differences:
\end{enumerate}

\begin{equation}\phantomsection\label{eq-stx}{V_{\text{STX}} = V \times |J|}\end{equation}

where \(J\) is the Jacobian determinant of the nonlinear transformation.

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}
\setcounter{enumi}{3}
\tightlist
\item
  \textbf{Residualized}: linear regression residuals after removing ICV
  association, with regression coefficients estimated from the pooled
  sample {[}O'Brien et al., 2011; Sanchis-Segura et al., 2020{]}:
\end{enumerate}

\begin{equation}\phantomsection\label{eq-res}{V_{\text{RES}} = V - \hat{\beta} \times (\text{ICV} - \overline{\text{ICV}})}\end{equation}

Residualization eliminates ICV-related variance and produces more
replicable sex differences compared to proportional methods.

\subsubsection{HVR Calculation}\label{hvr-calculation}

The hippocampal-to-ventricle ratio (HVR) was computed as:

\begin{equation}\phantomsection\label{eq-hvr}{\text{HVR} = \frac{\text{HC}_{\text{total}}}{\text{HC}_{\text{total}} + \text{LV}_{\text{total}}}}\end{equation}

where \(\text{HC}_{\text{total}}\) and \(\text{LV}_{\text{total}}\)
denote bilateral sums (left + right) of hippocampal and lateral
ventricular volumes, respectively. HVR is bounded between 0 and 1, with
higher values indicating larger hippocampus relative to ventricles.
Because HVR is a ratio of two head-size-dependent structures, no further
head-size adjustment was applied.

\subsection{Cognitive Assessment}\label{cognitive-assessment}

\subsubsection{Test Battery}\label{test-battery}

Cognitive data were collected at the imaging visit using a
touchscreen-based battery of 12 indicators spanning memory, processing
speed, executive function, and reasoning domains (Table S1):

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}
\tightlist
\item
  \textbf{Pair matching -- average time} (mean response time;
  inverse-coded)
\item
  \textbf{Pair matching -- average errors} (mean incorrect responses;
  inverse-coded)
\item
  \textbf{Numeric memory} (maximum digits recalled)
\item
  \textbf{Prospective memory} (recall timing: 0 = incorrect, 1 = second
  attempt, 2 = first attempt)
\item
  \textbf{Fluid intelligence} (total score)
\item
  \textbf{Matrix pattern recognition} (puzzles solved)
\item
  \textbf{Trail making -- numeric} (completion time; inverse-coded)
\item
  \textbf{Trail making -- alphanumeric} (completion time; inverse-coded)
\item
  \textbf{Tower rearranging} (correct puzzles)
\item
  \textbf{Reaction time} (mean time to identify matches; inverse-coded)
\item
  \textbf{Symbol digit substitution -- correct} (correct responses)
\item
  \textbf{Symbol digit substitution -- attempts} (total attempts)
\end{enumerate}

Missing cognitive data were handled using full information maximum
likelihood (FIML) estimation, which uses all available data under a
missing-at-random assumption.

\subsubsection{Factor Structure: Bifactor
CFA}\label{factor-structure-bifactor-cfa}

Cognitive indicators were modeled using a bifactor confirmatory factor
analysis (CFA) {[}Sudlow et al., 2015{]} with three latent factors. The
general factor (\emph{g}) loaded on all 12 indicators. The
memory-specific factor (Memory\textsubscript{s}, orthogonal to \emph{g})
loaded on pair matching, numeric memory, and prospective memory. The
processing speed-specific factor (Speed\textsubscript{s}, orthogonal to
\emph{g}) loaded on reaction time, trail making numeric, and symbol
digit substitution correct. Factor scales were set using the marker
variable method (first loading fixed to 1.0 for each factor). All latent
factors were constrained to be orthogonal, consistent with bifactor
model assumptions. Cross-loadings were fixed to zero. Residual
covariances were specified for indicator pairs sharing method variance
within the same test (pair matching time with errors; symbol digit
correct with attempts; trail making numeric with alphanumeric).

Models were estimated using maximum likelihood with robust standard
errors (MLR) in lavaan {[}Rosseel, 2012{]}.

\subsection{Statistical Analyses}\label{statistical-analyses}

\subsubsection{Analysis 1: HVR Self-Normalizing
Properties}\label{analysis-1-hvr-self-normalizing-properties}

To evaluate whether HVR reduces head-size confounding, we computed
Pearson correlations between ICV and each brain measure (HC, LV, HVR) as
well as between ICV and each head-size adjusted HC volume (proportions,
stereotaxic, residualized). We expected a self-normalizing measure to
show reduced correlation with ICV compared to unadjusted volumes.

\subsubsection{Analysis 2: Sex Differences in Brain
Measures}\label{analysis-2-sex-differences-in-brain-measures}

Sex differences were quantified using Cohen's d with 95\% confidence
intervals {[}Cohen, 2013{]}, with positive d indicating females
\textgreater{} males by our coding convention. Effect sizes were
computed for all ROI x adjustment method combinations in the full sample
(N = 27,680).

To isolate sex effects from head-size confounding, we created a matched
subsample where females and males were paired on age (\(\pm 1\) year)
and ICV (\(\pm 25\) cc, approximately 0.3 SD). Matching used the MatchIt
package (v4.7.2; Ho et al. {[}2011{]}) with optimal 1:1 matching without
replacement (\texttt{method\ =\ "optimal"},
\texttt{distance\ =\ "mahalanobis"}), with calipers enforcing tolerance
bounds. This yielded N = 4,750 matched pairs (total N = 9,500). We
reasoned that if sex differences in HC persist after matching on ICV,
they cannot be attributed solely to head-size confounding, and if HVR
shows similar effects in full and matched samples, this indicates HVR is
independent of head size.

\subsubsection{Analysis 3: Age x Sex
Interactions}\label{analysis-3-age-x-sex-interactions}

Linear regression models tested whether cross-sectional age-related
patterns differed by sex:

\begin{equation}\phantomsection\label{eq-age-sex}{Y_i = \beta_0 + \beta_{\text{Age}} \cdot \text{Age}_i + \beta_{\text{Sex}} \cdot \text{Sex}_i + \beta_{\text{Age} \times \text{Sex}} \cdot \text{Age}_i \times \text{Sex}_i + \varepsilon_i}\end{equation}

where \(Y\) is the brain measure, Sex is coded as a binary indicator
(Female = reference), and
\(\varepsilon_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)\). A significant
\(\beta_{\text{Age} \times \text{Sex}}\) indicates different age slopes
between sexes. Sex-specific slopes were derived as
\(\hat{\beta}_{\text{Age}}\) for females and
\(\hat{\beta}_{\text{Age}} + \hat{\beta}_{\text{Age} \times \text{Sex}}\)
for males, and their ratio quantified the relative rate of
cross-sectional age-related differences. Separate models were fit for
HC\textsubscript{RES} and LV\textsubscript{RES} (both residualized
against ICV; Equation~\ref{eq-res}) and HVR (self-normalizing;
Equation~\ref{eq-hvr}). While age trajectories may be non-linear (as
captured by the GAMLSS normative models below), linear interaction
models were used here to provide interpretable summary statistics for
sex-differential aging rates.

\subsubsection{Analysis 4: Normative
Modeling}\label{analysis-4-normative-modeling}

Sex-specific normative centile curves were estimated using Generalized
Additive Models for Location, Scale, and Shape (GAMLSS) {[}Bethlehem et
al., 2022; Rigby and Stasinopoulos, 2005{]}. For HC and LV (unadjusted):

\begin{equation}\phantomsection\label{eq-gamlss-hc}{\mu = f_\mu(\text{Age}) + \beta_{\text{ICV}} \cdot \text{ICV} + \beta_{\text{Edu}} \cdot \text{Education} + u_{\text{site}}}\end{equation}

For HVR (self-normalizing, no ICV term needed):

\begin{equation}\phantomsection\label{eq-gamlss-hvr}{\mu = f_\mu(\text{Age}) + \beta_{\text{Edu}} \cdot \text{Education} + u_{\text{site}}}\end{equation}

where \(f_\mu(\cdot)\) denotes a cubic spline smooth for the location
parameter and \(u_{\text{site}}\) denotes random intercepts for scanner
site. The scale parameter was also modeled as a function of age,
\(\sigma = f_\sigma(\text{Age})\), allowing variance to change across
the age range. Model families (Gaussian and Box-Cox Cole and Green;
BCCG) were compared using AIC, with BIC as secondary criterion. The BCCG
distribution was used when flexible modeling of location (\(\mu\)),
scale (\(\sigma\)), and skewness (\(\nu\)) was warranted; shape
parameters beyond \(\sigma\) were held constant to maintain parsimony.
Centile curves (5th, 10th, 25th, 50th, 75th, 90th, 95th) were generated
for ages 45--82 by sex.

Data were split into 80\% training and 20\% test sets, stratified by sex
and 5-year age bins. Distribution families were selected on training
data and evaluated on the held-out test set by comparing observed
vs.~expected proportions across centiles. Final models were then fit on
the complete cross-sectional sample using the selected distribution
family. Model calibration was additionally assessed using the
longitudinal subsample (N = 2,935; see Data Source and Participants).
Because this subsample comprises the same participants as the primary
cross-sectional sample with an additional follow-up session, it tests
temporal stability rather than independent validation. Test-retest
reliability was quantified using Pearson correlation and intraclass
correlation coefficient (ICC, two-way random, absolute agreement).

Two sets of GAMLSS models were generated: (1) site-controlled models
including random site intercepts (\(u_{\text{site}}\)) for optimal
within-UKB accuracy, and (2) transfer models without site effects for
application to external cohorts where site is unknown or not comparable.
Transfer models used identical distribution families but omitted the
random site term, following recommendations for cross-study normative
modeling.

\subsubsection{Analysis 5: Brain-Cognition SEM and Measurement
Invariance}\label{analysis-5-brain-cognition-sem-and-measurement-invariance}

Joint measurement-structural equation models tested associations between
brain measures and cognitive factors. These analyses were restricted to
the England subsample (N = 25,172; see Data Source and Participants)
because the Index of Multiple Deprivation (IMDP), used as a
socioeconomic covariate, is not comparable across UK countries. All
models were estimated in lavaan {[}Rosseel, 2012{]}.

\paragraph{Measurement Invariance}\label{measurement-invariance}

Comparing brain-cognition associations across sexes requires that the
cognitive measurement model is equivalent across groups. Measurement
invariance of the bifactor structure (see Factor Structure: Bifactor
CFA) was therefore tested following standard multi-group CFA procedures
{[}Putnick and Bornstein, 2016{]}. Four nested models were tested: (1)
configural invariance (same factor structure), (2) metric invariance
(equal loadings), (3) scalar invariance (equal intercepts), and (4)
strict invariance (equal residuals). Invariance was evaluated using
\(\Delta\)CFI \(\leq\) 0.01 {[}Cheung and Rensvold, 2002{]} and
\(\Delta\)RMSEA \(\leq\) 0.015 {[}Chen, 2007{]}. At minimum, metric
invariance is required to compare structural paths across groups.

\paragraph{SEM Specifications}\label{sem-specifications}

Given adequate measurement invariance, three SEM models were estimated,
each combining the bifactor cognitive measurement model with structural
paths from a brain measure to all three cognitive factors. Brain
measures entered as observed (manifest) variables: HC (bilateral
hippocampal volume, sum of left and right), HVR (Equation~\ref{eq-hvr}),
and HC\textsubscript{RES} (HC residualized against ICV within sex,
yielding r \(\approx\) 0 with ICV by construction). The structural
component of each model took the following form:

\textbf{Model 1 (HC):}

\begin{equation}\phantomsection\label{eq-sem-hc}{\begin{aligned}
\text{HC} &= \beta_{\text{Age}} \cdot \text{Age} + \beta_{\text{ICV}} \cdot \text{ICV} + \beta_{\text{Edu}} \cdot \text{Edu} + \beta_{\text{IMDP}} \cdot \text{IMDP} + \zeta_{\text{HC}} \\
\eta_k &= \beta_{k,\text{HC}} \cdot \text{HC} + \beta_{k,\text{Age}} \cdot \text{Age} + \beta_{k,\text{ICV}} \cdot \text{ICV} + \beta_{k,\text{Edu}} \cdot \text{Edu} + \beta_{k,\text{IMDP}} \cdot \text{IMDP} + \zeta_k
\end{aligned}}\end{equation}

\textbf{Model 2 (HVR):}

\begin{equation}\phantomsection\label{eq-sem-hvr}{\begin{aligned}
\text{HVR} &= \beta_{\text{Age}} \cdot \text{Age} + \beta_{\text{Age}^2} \cdot \text{Age}^2 + \beta_{\text{ICV}} \cdot \text{ICV} \\
  &\quad + \beta_{\text{Edu}} \cdot \text{Edu} + \beta_{\text{IMDP}} \cdot \text{IMDP} + \zeta_{\text{HVR}} \\
\eta_k &= \beta_{k,\text{HVR}} \cdot \text{HVR} + \beta_{k,\text{Age}} \cdot \text{Age} + \beta_{k,\text{Age}^2} \cdot \text{Age}^2 \\
  &\quad + \beta_{k,\text{ICV}} \cdot \text{ICV} + \beta_{k,\text{Edu}} \cdot \text{Edu} + \beta_{k,\text{IMDP}} \cdot \text{IMDP} + \zeta_k
\end{aligned}}\end{equation}

\textbf{Model 3 (HC\textsubscript{RES}):}

\begin{equation}\phantomsection\label{eq-sem-hcres}{\begin{aligned}
\text{HC}_{\text{RES}} &= \beta_{\text{Age}} \cdot \text{Age} + \beta_{\text{Age}^2} \cdot \text{Age}^2 + \beta_{\text{ICV}} \cdot \text{ICV} \\
  &\quad + \beta_{\text{Edu}} \cdot \text{Edu} + \beta_{\text{IMDP}} \cdot \text{IMDP} + \zeta_{\text{HC}_{\text{RES}}} \\
\eta_k &= \beta_{k,\text{HC}_{\text{RES}}} \cdot \text{HC}_{\text{RES}} + \beta_{k,\text{Age}} \cdot \text{Age} + \beta_{k,\text{Age}^2} \cdot \text{Age}^2 \\
  &\quad + \beta_{k,\text{ICV}} \cdot \text{ICV} + \beta_{k,\text{Edu}} \cdot \text{Edu} + \beta_{k,\text{IMDP}} \cdot \text{IMDP} + \zeta_k
\end{aligned}}\end{equation}

where \(\eta_k \in \{g, \text{Memory}_s, \text{Speed}_s\}\) denotes
latent cognitive factors and \(\zeta\) denotes residual terms. A
quadratic age term was included in the HVR and HC\textsubscript{RES}
models but not in the HC model, where the combination of raw hippocampal
volume, ICV, and quadratic age produced multicollinearity. Age was
centered within sex groups before squaring. All structural variables
were z-standardized within sex groups to address scale differences.
Covariates are summarized in Table S2.

The HC\textsubscript{RES} model serves as a benchmark representing the
methodological standard for removing head-size confounding. Comparing
HVR to HC\textsubscript{RES} tests whether HVR provides similar
cognitive associations while being self-normalizing and capturing
additional variance through its incorporation of ventricular dynamics.
The proportions and stereotaxic adjustment methods were excluded due to
concerns about biologically implausible associations and residual ICV
correlation, respectively {[}Wang et al., 2024{]}.

\paragraph{Estimation and Inference}\label{estimation-and-inference}

Two estimation approaches were used. Pooled models combined all
participants, included sex as a covariate, and were estimated using
robust maximum likelihood (MLR) with cluster-robust standard errors to
account for scanner site. Multi-group models estimated separate
structural parameters for females and males, allowing sex differences in
brain-cognition paths to be directly tested; these were estimated using
maximum likelihood with bootstrap confidence intervals (2,000 resamples)
{[}Cumming and Finch, 2005{]}. Missing cognitive data were handled using
full information maximum likelihood (FIML) in both approaches. Sex
differences in path coefficients were evaluated by comparing bootstrap
confidence intervals across groups.

\subsubsection{Analysis 6: Sensitivity
Analyses}\label{analysis-6-sensitivity-analyses}

Three sensitivity analyses assessed the robustness of sex difference
findings to analytical choices.

\paragraph{Site effects}\label{site-effects}

Scanner site (UK Biobank assessment centre) can introduce systematic
variance in volumetric measurements due to differences in scanner
hardware, software versions, or acquisition protocols. Site effects were
quantified using the intraclass correlation coefficient:

\begin{equation}\phantomsection\label{eq-icc}{\text{ICC} = \frac{\sigma^2_{\text{site}}}{\sigma^2_{\text{site}} + \sigma^2_{\text{residual}}}}\end{equation}

Site-adjusted sex difference effect sizes were computed using linear
mixed-effects models with a fixed effect for sex and a random intercept
for scanner site:

\begin{equation}\phantomsection\label{eq-site-adj}{Y_i = \beta_0 + \beta_{\text{Sex}} \cdot \text{Sex}_i + u_{\text{site}(i)} + \varepsilon_i}\end{equation}

where \(u_{\text{site}} \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2_{\text{site}})\)
and \(\varepsilon_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2_{\text{residual}})\).
Cohen's d was approximated as
\(\hat{\beta}_{\text{Sex}} / \hat{\sigma}_{\text{residual}}\), with
confidence intervals derived from the Wald approximation.

\paragraph{Hemisphere comparison}\label{hemisphere-comparison}

Left and right hippocampus and lateral ventricle volumes (residualized
against ICV) were analyzed separately to assess whether sex differences
are lateralized. Cohen's d was computed for each hemisphere
independently, with effect size comparisons based on overlapping 95\%
confidence intervals. Non-overlapping intervals indicate a statistically
significant difference in effect magnitude between hemispheres.

\paragraph{Inclusion of psychiatric
diagnoses}\label{inclusion-of-psychiatric-diagnoses}

The primary analysis excluded participants with ICD-10 diagnoses in the
F (mental and behavioral disorders), G (neurological), or Q0 (congenital
brain malformations) categories. To assess robustness, sex difference
analyses were repeated in an expanded sensitivity sample that included
participants with F-code diagnoses (F00-F99) while still excluding G and
Q0 codes. The difference in Cohen's d between primary and sensitivity
samples (\(\Delta d\) = \(d_{\text{primary}}\) -
\(d_{\text{sensitivity}}\)) quantified the impact of psychiatric
exclusion criteria. Small differences indicate that findings generalize
to a less restrictively selected sample and are not driven by exclusion
of psychiatric comorbidity.

\subsection{Sex Differences in Brain Volumes (Analysis
2)}\label{sex-differences-in-brain-volumes-analysis-2}

Sex differences varied across brain measures and adjustment methods (N =
27,680; Figure~\ref{fig-effect-sizes}).

\subsubsection{Hippocampal Volume}\label{hippocampal-volume}

HC sex differences reversed direction depending on the adjustment
method. Unadjusted volumes showed d = -0.89 {[}95\% CI: -0.91, -0.86{]},
indicating males had larger absolute volumes. Proportions adjustment
reversed this to d = 0.58 {[}0.56, 0.6{]} with females larger.
Stereotaxic adjustment yielded d = 0.59 {[}0.56, 0.61{]}, and
residualized volumes showed a negligible effect (d = 0.02 {[}0,
0.05{]}). The effect size range across methods spanned 1.47 standard
deviations.

\subsubsection{Lateral Ventricular
Volume}\label{lateral-ventricular-volume}

Unlike HC, lateral ventricular sex differences were consistently
negative (males \textgreater{} females) across all adjustment methods,
though effect magnitudes were attenuated by head-size correction.
Unadjusted LV showed d = -0.84 {[}-0.86, -0.82{]}, similar in magnitude
to unadjusted HC. After adjustment, all methods retained the male
advantage: proportions d = -0.34 {[}-0.37, -0.32{]}, stereotaxic d =
-0.31 {[}-0.33, -0.28{]}, and residualized d = -0.16 {[}-0.19, -0.14{]}.
The directional consistency across methods contrasts with the reversals
observed for HC.

\subsubsection{HVR}\label{hvr}

HVR showed a consistent female advantage: d = 0.52 {[}95\% CI: 0.49,
0.54{]}. The combination of males having relatively smaller hippocampi
(after head-size adjustment) and larger ventricles produces this female
advantage in HVR. Distribution overlap between sexes across adjustment
methods is shown in Figure S1.

3)}\label{age-x-sex-interactions-analysis-3}

Cross-sectional age patterns (N = 27,680) differed by sex
(Figure~\ref{fig-age-trajectory}). For HVR, the age × sex interaction
was significant (\(\beta\) = -0.001, SE = 0, t = -16.58, p = 2e-61),
with males showing 1.66× steeper cross-sectional age-related
differences. The female HVR advantage \emph{widened} with age---effect
sizes increased from d = 0.23 at ages 45-55 to d = 0.66 at ages 75+.

showed males with 1.63× steeper cross-sectional differences (p =
3.1e-10). LV showed the largest sex disparity, with males exhibiting
1.87× steeper ventricular expansion (p = 4.6e-80). The greater sex
disparity in ventricular trajectories compared to hippocampal
trajectories explains the intermediate HVR ratio (1.66×), as HVR
captures both processes.

\section{Discussion}\label{discussion}

\subsection{Summary of Findings}\label{summary-of-findings}

In 27,680 UK Biobank participants, we evaluated HVR as a
self-normalizing biomarker that bypasses conventional head-size
correction. HVR showed weak negative correlation with ICV (r = -0.252),
representing a 58\% reduction in ICV dependence compared to unadjusted
HC; the residual negative correlation may reflect ventricular scaling
rather than incomplete normalization. Hippocampal sex differences ranged
from d = -0.89 (males larger) to d = 0.58 (females larger) depending
solely on the adjustment method, whereas HVR consistently showed females
higher than males (d = 0.52), an effect replicated in ICV-matched
samples. HVR predicted general cognitive ability comparably to
hippocampal volume (overlapping confidence intervals). Males showed
steeper age-related HVR differences (1.66× female slope), with the
female advantage widening at older ages (d = 0.23 at 45-55y to d = 0.66
at 75+y).

\subsection{HVR Self-Normalizing Properties (Analysis
1)}\label{hvr-self-normalizing-properties-analysis-1-1}

HVR achieves head-size independence through its mathematical
construction: both numerator (HC) and denominator (HC + LV) scale with
head size, so their ratio largely normalizes out this confound
{[}Schoemaker et al., 2019{]}. In two healthy aging cohorts (N = 50 and
N = 88), Schoemaker et al. {[}2019{]} demonstrated that HVR yielded
stronger correlations with both age and memory performance than absolute
hippocampal volume, supporting its utility as an integrity index. In the
present sample, HVR achieved a 58\% reduction in ICV correlation
compared to unadjusted HC (r = -0.252 vs.~r = 0.605). This
self-normalizing approach complements other linear ratio measures used
in clinical neuroimaging, such as the Evans' index for ventricular
assessment {[}Currà et al., 2019{]}, and avoids the well-documented
pitfalls of proportional ICV correction, which produces biologically
implausible results when brain structures scale non-isometrically with
head size {[}Pintzka et al., 2015; Sanchis-Segura et al., 2020; Wang et
al., 2024{]}.

The residual negative correlation between HVR and ICV (r = -0.252)
likely reflects ventricular scaling rather than incomplete
normalization. Lateral ventricular volume shows positive correlation
with ICV (r = 0.462), and the relative size of the ventricles increases
with increasing ICV {[}Pintzka et al., 2015{]}. This disproportionate
scaling is confirmed by allometric analyses showing that hippocampal
volume scales hypoallometrically with ICV (\(\alpha\) = 0.60), while the
lateral ventricles scale near-isometrically (\(\alpha\) \(\approx\) 1.0;
Jong et al. {[}2017{]}). Since HVR = HC/(HC+LV), and larger heads have
disproportionately larger ventricles relative to hippocampal volume,
larger ICV naturally produces lower HVR. The negative HVR-ICV
correlation thus mirrors the positive LV-ICV relationship, capturing the
non-isometric scaling of ventricular volume with head size. Unlike
residualization, which removes \emph{all} ICV association by
construction (r \(\approx\) 0), HVR preserves morphological variation
related to how ventricular space scales with overall brain size. This
may be advantageous for applications where the distinction between
``large head with typical ventricles'' and ``large head with enlarged
ventricles'' carries clinical significance.

\subsection{Sex Differences in Brain Measures (Analysis
2)}\label{sex-differences-in-brain-measures-analysis-2}

\subsubsection{Hippocampal Sex Differences as Methodological
Artifacts}\label{hippocampal-sex-differences-as-methodological-artifacts}

The reversal of HC sex differences across adjustment methods indicates
that these differences reflect head-size confounding rather than
underlying biology. Effect sizes ranged from d = -0.89 (males larger,
unadjusted) to d = 0.58 (females larger, proportions-adjusted)---a swing
of 1.47 standard deviations depending solely on analytical choices. This
reversal is well-replicated: Pintzka et al. {[}2015{]} found unadjusted
hippocampal volumes significantly larger in males that reversed to
females larger under proportional correction; Voevodskaya et al.
{[}2014{]} observed the identical pattern in the PIVUS cohort (N = 406);
and Fjell et al. {[}2009{]}, in a multisample study of 1,143 healthy
participants, reported that unadjusted hippocampal volumes favoring
males (z = −0.72) reversed to favor females when corrected for total
brain volume (z = 0.13). In all cases, residualization eliminated the
sex difference entirely.

This over-correction reflects allometric scaling: the medial temporal
lobe (including hippocampus) scales at \(\alpha\) = 0.60 relative to ICV
{[}95\% CI: 0.56-0.64{]}, not isometrically {[}Jong et al., 2017{]}, and
83\% of brain measures in the UK Biobank show similar non-isometric
scaling {[}Williams et al., 2021{]}. Because the regression of
hippocampal volume on ICV does not pass through the origin, dividing by
ICV systematically overestimates relative volume in smaller heads
{[}Pintzka et al., 2015; Voevodskaya et al., 2014{]}. The near-zero
effect with residualization (d = 0.022, 95\% CI crossing zero) suggests
that after appropriate statistical control, hippocampal volume does not
meaningfully differ between sexes.

These results converge with other large-scale studies. Ritchie et al.
{[}2018{]} reported that unadjusted hippocampal sex differences in 5,216
UK Biobank participants (d \(\approx\) −0.55) dropped to near-zero
(\(|\)d\(|\) \textless{} 0.03) after regression-based adjustment for
total brain volume. ICV-matched UK Biobank analyses similarly show sex
effects approaching zero {[}Brzezinski-Rittner et al., 2025{]},
brain-based sex classification drops from \textgreater80\% to
\textasciitilde60\% after head-size adjustment {[}Sanchis-Segura et al.,
2020{]}, and individual brains are better characterized as mosaics of
features than categorically sexed {[}Joel et al., 2018{]}. While total
hippocampal volume sex differences appear methodologically determined,
sex-specific developmental trajectories in hippocampal subregions {[}Mu
et al., 2020{]} and structural connectivity patterns {[}Ingalhalikar et
al., 2014{]} suggest that meaningful differences may exist at finer
spatial scales.

\subsubsection{Lateral Ventricle Sex
Differences}\label{lateral-ventricle-sex-differences}

Unlike hippocampal volume, lateral ventricles showed males
\textgreater{} females across all adjustment methods
(Figure~\ref{fig-effect-sizes}). This directional consistency is
well-established: unadjusted ventricular volumes are larger in males
across multiple cohorts {[}Fjell et al., 2009; Pintzka et al., 2015;
Voevodskaya et al., 2014{]}, and---critically---the male advantage
persists or even increases after head-size correction. Fjell et al.
{[}2009{]} reported that the ventricular sex difference (z = −0.32
unadjusted) actually increased after total brain volume correction (z =
−0.56), and males show 28--128\% greater risk of abnormal ventricular
enlargement indices across age {[}Currà et al., 2019{]}. Under
proportional adjustment, ventricles remain larger in males
because---unlike the hippocampus---the LV-ICV regression has a negative
y-intercept, meaning proportional correction under-corrects rather than
over-corrects ventricular volumes {[}Pintzka et al., 2015; Voevodskaya
et al., 2014{]}. This mathematical property explains why proportional
adjustment reverses the hippocampal sex difference (positive y-intercept
→ over-correction) while preserving the ventricular sex difference
(negative y-intercept → under-correction), with Pintzka et al.
{[}2015{]} demonstrating that the sign of the y-intercept predicted the
direction of proportional sex differences with 100\% accuracy.

Smaller studies have reported that residualization eliminates
ventricular sex differences {[}Fjell et al., 2009; Voevodskaya et al.,
2014{]}, though Pintzka et al. {[}2015{]} found that inferior lateral
ventricle differences persisted even after residualization. In our
substantially larger sample (N = 27,680), residualized LV sex
differences remained significant (d = -0.16), suggesting that the null
findings in prior studies may reflect insufficient power. The consistent
male ventricular advantage across adjustment methods contrasts sharply
with the reversals observed for HC, indicating that this sex difference
is not a head-size artifact. Since HVR = HC/(HC+LV), the female HVR
advantage arises from two converging factors: relatively larger
head-size-adjusted hippocampi and relatively smaller ventricles. This
dual sensitivity makes HVR particularly informative for sex differences
in cross-sectional brain aging patterns.

\subsubsection{Matched-Pair Comparison and
HVR}\label{matched-pair-comparison-and-hvr}

The matched-pair analysis (Table~\ref{tbl-matched}) provides the
strongest evidence for HVR's validity as a head-size-independent
measure. Matching on age and ICV has been advocated as a ``gold standard
benchmark'' for disentangling sex from brain size {[}Brzezinski-Rittner
et al., 2025{]}, as it eliminates head-size variation without the
mathematical assumptions of statistical correction {[}Nordenskjöld et
al., 2015; Voevodskaya et al., 2014{]}. When females and males were
matched on age (\(\pm 1\) year) and ICV (\(\pm 25\) cc), the HC sex
difference collapsed to d = -0.038 (effectively zero), while HVR
retained a significant female advantage (d = 0.183). This hippocampal
convergence replicates prior matched-sample findings: Pintzka et al.
{[}2015{]} found non-significant HC differences in 152 ICV-matched
pairs, and Voevodskaya et al. {[}2014{]} reported that no hippocampal
differences remained in their ICV-matched subsample. Conversely, males
retained significantly larger ventricles (d = -0.2) even after ICV
matching---consistent with both Pintzka et al. {[}2015{]} and
Voevodskaya et al. {[}2014{]}, who found that ventricular sex
differences (third ventricle in both studies) survived strict ICV
matching when hippocampal differences did not. The convergence of
residualized HC and HVR effect sizes in matched samples provides
converging evidence that both approaches successfully remove head-size
confounding, though through different mechanisms: residualization
statistically removes ICV variance, while HVR leverages the shared
scaling of hippocampal and ventricular volumes.

The consistent female HVR advantage across full (d = 0.52) and matched
(d = 0.18) samples supports a sex difference in the
hippocampus-to-ventricle balance. The 65\% reduction in effect size from
full to matched samples indicates that most of the HVR sex difference is
attributable to head-size differences, but a significant residual effect
persists. This residual could reflect greater relative hippocampal
volume in females after accounting for ventricular size, smaller
relative ventricular volume in females (as confirmed by the
matched-sample analysis showing LV d = -0.2 favoring males even after
ICV matching), or sex differences in the relationship between
hippocampal size and ventricular size across ages.

\subsection{Age × Sex Interactions (Analysis
3)}\label{age-sex-interactions-analysis-3}

The steeper male HVR differences with age suggests sex differences in
age-related patterns of hippocampal and ventricular volumes. The age ×
sex interaction analyses revealed a consistent pattern across all
measures: males showed 1.66× steeper HVR cross-sectional differences (p
= 2e-61), 1.63× steeper HC differences (p = 3.1e-10), and 1.87× steeper
LV differences (p = 4.6e-80). Steeper male subcortical volume decline is
consistent with Ritchie et al. {[}2018{]}, who reported significant age
× sex interactions for hippocampal volume in 5,216 UK Biobank
participants. For ventricular volumes, Fjell et al. {[}2009{]} found
that global CSF expansion showed a steeper age correlation in males (r =
0.50) than females (r = 0.36) after ICV correction.

The greater sex disparity in ventricular age-related differences (1.87×)
compared to hippocampal differences (1.63×) explains why HVR shows
intermediate ratios (1.66×): HVR captures both processes, and the
steeper male ventricular differences amplify the already steeper
hippocampal differences. However, these interactions should be
interpreted cautiously: Brzezinski-Rittner et al. {[}2025{]} found that
hippocampal age × sex interactions present in the full UK Biobank sample
disappeared entirely in a TIV-matched subsample (N = 11,294), suggesting
that some apparent sex-differential aging trajectories may be
mathematical byproducts of initial brain size differences. Whether HVR
age × sex interactions similarly reflect head-size confounding or
genuine biological sex differences remains to be tested in matched
longitudinal designs.

Several mechanisms may explain these sex-differential patterns.
Cumulative estrogen exposure is protective: women with longer
reproductive spans show younger brain ages (0.32 years per year of
earlier menarche; Luders et al. {[}2025{]}), and earlier HRT initiation
near menopause is associated with less evident brain aging in APOE ε4
carriers {[}Lange et al., 2020{]}. Estrogen promotes hippocampal
neurogenesis, synaptic plasticity, mitochondrial function, and
neuroinflammation reduction {[}Diaz Brinton, 2008; Jett et al., 2022;
Sundermann et al., 2016{]}, with the ``critical period hypothesis''
proposing that continuous exposure is necessary for neuroprotection.
Menopause status may also moderate sex differences in tau pathology
burden, with amyloid-related atrophy more strongly affecting women---an
association likely mediated by higher tau accumulation {[}Bachmann et
al., 2023{]}.

Males also show earlier ventricular abnormality signatures, with
ventricular enlargement accelerating in the seventies and preferential
frontal horn involvement {[}Currà et al., 2019{]}. This earlier male
ventricular expansion is consistent with the 1.66× steeper male HVR
decline observed here, suggesting that accelerated ventricular
expansion---rather than differential hippocampal aging alone---drives
the sex-differential HVR trajectory. The prognostic relevance of such
trajectory differences is supported by evidence that steeper hippocampal
volume loss during MCI predicts conversion to Alzheimer's dementia
{[}Rajagopal et al., 2024{]}.

However, the female advantage is not universal: female APOE ε4 carriers
show 2.3× faster hippocampal volume loss than male carriers in aMCI
{[}Park et al., 2025{]}, indicating that genetic risk can modulate or
reverse typical sex-differential patterns. Other large studies have
found no significant sex differences in hippocampal age slopes {[}Fjell
et al., 2009; Pintzka et al., 2015{]}, underscoring that the presence
and magnitude of age × sex interactions depends on sample size, age
range, and head-size adjustment method. These cross-sectional patterns
cannot establish true individual trajectories and may be confounded by
cohort effects or differential survival. Longitudinal validation is
essential.

\subsection{Normative Modeling (Analysis
4)}\label{normative-modeling-analysis-4-1}

The sex-specific centile curves (Figure~\ref{fig-normative-centiles})
extend the growing literature on normative brain charts {[}Bethlehem et
al., 2022; Dima et al., 2022; Ge et al., 2024{]} by providing the first
sex-specific reference values for HVR alongside its component measures.
While prior work has generated sex-stratified centiles for hippocampal
volume {[}Dima et al., 2022; Ge et al., 2024{]} and global brain tissue
volumes {[}Bethlehem et al., 2022{]}, none have modeled the
hippocampus-to-ventricle ratio. Our use of GAMLSS aligns with the
methodological framework adopted by Bethlehem et al. {[}2022{]} and Dima
et al. {[}2022{]}, which allows flexible modeling of both non-linear age
trends in the median and age-related changes in between-subject
variability---a critical advantage over conventional models given the
increasing variance observed at older ages, particularly for ventricular
volumes.

Sex-specific norms are essential to avoid misclassification: a male at
the 50th percentile for his sex would fall below the 25th percentile
using female norms, potentially leading to false-positive concerns. This
is consistent with recommendations from Williams et al. {[}2021{]}, who
found that males exhibit greater variance in volumetric measures and
concluded that ``investigations of neuroanatomical deviance should
always take sex into account,'' and with Ge et al. {[}2024{]}, who
developed sex-specific models for 150 brain measures because sex
accounts for a considerable proportion of morphological variance. For
HC, after residualization, sex differences in hippocampal centiles are
minimal, consistent with the near-zero Cohen's d for residualized
volumes, supporting the use of combined norms when proper ICV adjustment
is applied. For LV, both sexes show larger ventricular volumes at older
ages, but cross-sectional patterns and variability differ, with males
showing greater ventricular variability at older ages---potentially
reflecting greater heterogeneity in aging-related patterns.

The GAMLSS calibration plots (Figure S2) confirm that these centile
estimates are well-calibrated, with observed proportions closely
matching expected proportions in held-out test data. Individualized
centile scores have demonstrated clinical utility in detecting
case-control differences across neurological disorders and in
quantitatively predicting diagnostic transition from MCI to Alzheimer's
disease {[}Bethlehem et al., 2022; Dima et al., 2022{]}. Values below
the 5th centile may warrant clinical follow-up, and longitudinal decline
of more than one major centile band (e.g., 50th to 25th) may indicate
accelerating age-related change.

\subsection{Brain-Cognition Associations (Analysis
5)}\label{brain-cognition-associations-analysis-5-1}

\subsubsection{Measurement Invariance}\label{measurement-invariance-2}

The successful establishment of strict measurement invariance for the
cognitive bifactor model (Table S12) is a prerequisite for the
brain-cognition analyses. Without measurement invariance, apparent sex
differences in brain-cognition paths could reflect measurement artifacts
rather than true structural differences {[}Chen, 2007; Putnick and
Bornstein, 2016{]}. The \(\Delta\)CFI \(\leq\) 0.01 and \(\Delta\)RMSEA
\(\leq\) 0.015 at each invariance level {[}Chen, 2007; Cheung and
Rensvold, 2002{]} confirms that the same cognitive constructs (\emph{g},
Memory\textsubscript{s}, Speed\textsubscript{s}) exist in both sexes,
that factor loadings are equivalent (meaning the tests measure
constructs with equal precision), that intercepts are equivalent
(allowing valid comparison of factor means), and that residual variances
are equivalent (confirming comparable reliability). This finding
validates the use of the same factor structure for both sexes in the SEM
analyses and supports the comparability of path coefficients across
groups.

\subsubsection{Pooled and Sex-Stratified
Paths}\label{pooled-and-sex-stratified-paths}

HVR predicted cognition as well as HC. Pooled model estimates with
bootstrap CIs showed: HC → \emph{g} \(\beta\) = 0.04 {[}0.023, 0.056{]};
HVR → \emph{g} \(\beta\) = 0.043 {[}0.027, 0.058{]};
HC\textsubscript{RES} → \emph{g} \(\beta\) = 0.028 {[}0.014, 0.042{]}.
HVR showed a numerically larger (though with overlapping CIs)
association with general cognition despite requiring no head-size
adjustment. These effect sizes are consistent with the small structural
brain-cognition associations typically observed in large healthy
samples: Ritchie et al. {[}2018{]} reported all brain-cognitive
correlations below r = 0.20 in 5,216 UK Biobank participants, Wang et
al. {[}2024{]} found adjusted hippocampal → fluid intelligence
coefficients of \(\beta\) = 0.055 in 41,964 participants, and
hippocampal volume-cognition associations in healthy aging cohorts are
often non-significant at smaller sample sizes {[}Bachmann et al., 2023;
Schoemaker et al., 2019{]}. The restricted cognitive range in healthy
volunteer samples likely attenuates these associations {[}Bachmann et
al., 2023{]}.

Multigroup analyses indicated sex differences in brain-cognition
associations. Residualized hippocampal volume (HC\textsubscript{RES})
was not significantly associated with cognition in males (\(\beta\) =
0.013, 95\% CI {[}-0.008, 0.035{]}, p = 0.227), while remaining
significant in females (\(\beta\) = 0.036, p \textless{} .001). This
female-selective hippocampal-cognition association is consistent with
Zheng et al. {[}2017{]}, who found that hippocampal gray matter volume
predicted associative memory in older women (r = 0.62) but not men, and
with Jockwitz et al. {[}2024{]}, who found that brain structure
predicted cognitive profiles more strongly in females than males. Raw HC
showed significant associations in both sexes but with a female
advantage (\(\beta\) = 0.048 vs.~0.027). However, Ritchie et al.
{[}2018{]} found no significant sex differences in brain-cognition
correlations for verbal-numerical reasoning and reaction time,
suggesting that sex-differential associations may be domain-specific.

HVR showed the opposite pattern: males exhibited \emph{stronger}
HVR-cognition associations (\(\beta\) = 0.045) than females (\(\beta\) =
0.036), though both reached significance. HVR captures the
hippocampus-to-ventricle balance, and the male-stronger association
suggests that this ratio may be more cognitively relevant in males,
perhaps because males show steeper cross-sectional HVR differences
(1.66× female rate) making individual differences more consequential.

For processing speed (Speed\textsubscript{s}), males showed strong HC
and HC\textsubscript{RES} associations (\(\beta\) = 0.068 and 0.057)
while females showed none---suggesting sex-differential neural
substrates for processing speed.

\subsubsection{Implications for the HVR
Thesis}\label{implications-for-the-hvr-thesis}

These sex-differential patterns support HVR in several ways. Unlike
HC\textsubscript{RES}, which loses predictive validity in males for
general cognition, HVR maintains significant associations in both sexes.
Schoemaker et al. {[}2019{]} similarly found that HVR explained more
variance in memory scores than absolute hippocampal volume (40\%
vs.~32\% in their first cohort), with HC failing to reach significance
as a predictor in models where HVR was significant. Fernandez-Lozano et
al. {[}2025{]} extended this finding to clinical populations, showing
that HVR associations with cognition were significant across healthy,
MCI, and AD groups and were comparable in magnitude to hippocampal
volume. Given that males show steeper cross-sectional HVR differences
with age and stronger HVR-cognition associations, HVR may be useful for
tracking brain aging in males. The differential patterns between
HC\textsubscript{RES} and HVR across sexes indicate HVR is not simply
``residualized HC by another means'' but captures distinct information
related to the hippocampus-ventricle coupling. The association with
general cognition (\emph{g}) rather than domain-specific factors may
reflect that lateral ventricular volume acts as a marker for widespread
tissue loss beyond the medial temporal lobe: Bachmann et al. {[}2023{]}
found that ventricular volume was exclusively associated with executive
function rather than episodic memory, suggesting that HVR's ventricular
component captures cognitive-relevant variance beyond hippocampal
integrity.

\subsubsection{Model Stability and Non-Linear Age
Effects}\label{model-stability-and-non-linear-age-effects}

SEM analyses indicated that raw hippocampal volume (HC) required a
linear-age-only specification because the combination of HC, ICV, and
age² produced multicollinearity (information matrix not invertible). In
contrast, both HVR and residualized HC (HC\textsubscript{RES})
successfully incorporated quadratic age terms, which were significant
predictors of both brain structure and cognition (all p \textless{}
.001). Quadratic age effects were: HVR \textasciitilde{} age² \(\beta\)
= -0.134, indicating steeper HVR differences at older ages;
HC\textsubscript{RES} \textasciitilde{} age² \(\beta\) = -0.075; g
\textasciitilde{} age² \(\beta\) = -0.078; and Speed\textsubscript{s}
\textasciitilde{} age² \(\beta\) = -0.046, with processing speed showing
the expected age vulnerability.

The stronger quadratic effect for HVR (\(\beta\) = -0.134) compared to
HC\textsubscript{RES} (\(\beta\) = -0.075) reflects that HVR captures
both hippocampal and ventricular age-related differences, which both
show steeper cross-sectional patterns at older ages. HVR detects
accelerating age-related patterns. Researchers using raw hippocampal
volumes in complex multivariate models may encounter convergence issues
or require simpler covariate structures, potentially missing non-linear
relationships. Self-normalizing measures like HVR, or statistically
residualized volumes, allow for non-linear modeling.

\subsection{Sensitivity Analyses (Analysis
6)}\label{sensitivity-analyses-analysis-6-1}

\subsubsection{Site Effects}\label{site-effects-1}

The low site ICCs (Table S17, all \textless{} 0.05) indicate that
scanner site contributed minimally to variance in brain measures. This
is consistent with UK Biobank's harmonized acquisition protocol across
sites {[}Alfaro-Almagro et al., 2018{]}. The stability of effect sizes
after site adjustment confirms that our findings are not driven by
site-specific biases.

Different analytical contexts employed appropriate site-handling
methods. Normative GAMLSS models used random intercepts for site to
allow site-specific baseline levels while pooling information across
sites. Sex difference analyses used linear mixed-effects models with
random site intercepts. Pooled SEM analyses used cluster-robust standard
errors grouped by site rather than random effects, as the FIML estimator
in lavaan does not support random effects. Multi-group SEM analyses used
robust maximum likelihood estimation without site clustering, as lavaan
does not support the combination of multi-group analysis with clustered
standard errors.

For HVR specifically, site effects could theoretically differentially
affect hippocampus and ventricle measurements. The low site ICC for HVR
suggests that the ratio construction does not amplify site-related
noise.

\subsubsection{Hemisphere Comparison}\label{hemisphere-comparison-2}

For individual volumes, sex differences were symmetrical across
hemispheres: left and right hippocampal effect sizes were nearly
identical (d = −0.14 and −0.16, overlapping CIs), as were ventricular
effect sizes (d = 0.48 bilaterally). This bilateral symmetry is
consistent with Fjell et al. {[}2009{]}, who found a non-significant
hemisphere × sex × structure interaction (p = 0.97) across 13 brain
volumes including hippocampus and lateral ventricles, and with Ritchie
et al. {[}2018{]}, who reported nearly identical left (d = −0.55) and
right (d = −0.54) hippocampal sex differences in the UK Biobank.

However, HVR showed a lateralized pattern, with a significant female
advantage in the right hemisphere (d = 0.05) but not the left (d =
−0.01). Because HVR combines two bilaterally symmetrical structures into
a ratio, this lateralization likely reflects subtle asymmetries in how
hippocampal and ventricular volumes interact within each hemisphere
rather than lateralized sex differences in either structure alone.
Ruigrok et al. {[}2014{]} noted that female volume increases tend to be
more pronounced in right limbic structures, which could contribute to a
right-lateralized HVR advantage. However, automated segmentation
algorithms show hemispheric biases---higher sensitivity for the right
hippocampus but better ventricular segmentation on the left
{[}Fernandez-Lozano et al., 2025{]}---which could produce artifactual
laterality in a derived ratio measure. The small magnitude of this
lateralization (d = 0.05 vs.~−0.01) and its potential methodological
origins suggest that bilateral HVR remains the most robust measure for
clinical application.

\subsubsection{Inclusion of Psychiatric
Diagnoses}\label{inclusion-of-psychiatric-diagnoses-1}

Including participants with psychiatric diagnoses (F-codes) resulted in
minimal change in effect sizes (\(\Delta\)d \textless{} 0.02; Table S19,
Figure S4), with all findings retaining the same direction and
significance. This stability is expected given the low prevalence of
psychiatric diagnoses in the UK Biobank, which reflects the
well-documented healthy volunteer selection bias of the cohort {[}Fry et
al., 2017; Ritchie et al., 2018{]}. While reduced hippocampal volume is
a shared feature across major psychiatric conditions {[}Brosch et al.,
2022; Schmaal et al., 2016{]}, the low base rates in this sample mean
that inclusion or exclusion of F-code participants lacks the statistical
weight to meaningfully shift population-level estimates. Nonetheless,
establishing robustness to psychiatric exclusion criteria is important
because the brain regions showing the largest sex differences
(hippocampus, amygdala) overlap with those implicated in sex-biased
psychiatric conditions, creating potential sex-by-disorder interactions
that could confound normative sex difference estimates {[}Ritchie et
al., 2018; Ruigrok et al., 2014{]}.

\subsection{Limitations}\label{limitations}

This study has limitations related to design, sample, and methodology.
The cross-sectional design cannot distinguish age effects from cohort
effects or establish individual change trajectories, and HVR conflates
two structures, precluding separation of hippocampal and ventricular
contributions to observed differences.

Sample characteristics impose additional constraints. UK Biobank
participants are healthier and more educated than the general population
{[}Fry et al., 2017{]}, limiting generalizability to clinical
populations. Some cognitive tests had incomplete data, and complete-case
analyses may introduce selection bias. APOE genotype was unavailable,
precluding examination of gene × sex interactions relevant to dementia
risk.

Several methodological considerations warrant mention. The study did not
include the power-corrected proportions allometric adjustment method
proposed by Sanchis-Segura et al. {[}2020{]}. Imaging site clustering
was not modeled in multigroup SEM because lavaan does not support the
combination of multigroup analysis with clustered standard errors
{[}Rosseel, 2012{]}. However, with only 4 imaging sites across
approximately 27,000 participants (average cluster size
\textgreater6,000), the design effect is minimal, and any residual site
effects would inflate standard errors equally across sexes, making sex
comparisons conservative rather than biased.

The bifactor model's memory-specific factor (Memory\textsubscript{s})
could not be reliably estimated in either sex (females: estimation
failure; males: all loadings non-significant, p \textgreater{} .90).
This ``factor collapse'' is common in bifactor models, occurring in
approximately 60\% of published applications {[}Burns et al., 2020; Eid
et al., 2017{]}, indicating that variance in memory test performance was
fully captured by the general cognitive factor (\emph{g}).
Brain-cognition paths involving Memory\textsubscript{s} were therefore
not interpretable and are not reported, though the processing speed
factor (Speed\textsubscript{s}) was well-identified in both sexes.

Ratios can exhibit non-normality, heteroscedasticity, and complex
interpretation when numerator and denominator correlate differently with
outcomes; while HVR showed approximately normal distributions in this
sample, these properties should be verified in other populations. All
brain-cognition associations were small (\(\beta\) = 0.02--0.04),
explaining less than 1\% of variance in cognitive performance. While
statistically significant given the large sample, the practical and
clinical significance of these associations remains to be established.

\subsection{Future Directions}\label{future-directions}

The current findings motivate four lines of follow-up work. First, the
UK Biobank repeat imaging subsample enables longitudinal validation of
whether HVR predicts individual-level cognitive decline. Second,
clinical validation in AD/MCI cohorts would assess HVR's diagnostic
utility compared to clinical populations studied previously
{[}Fernandez-Lozano et al., 2025{]}. Third, longitudinal data could
separate hippocampal and ventricular contributions to HVR changes, which
the cross-sectional ratio conflates. Fourth, examining whether HVR × sex
× APOE genotype predicts dementia conversion would address the gene ×
sex interactions that this study could not explore.

\subsection{Conclusions}\label{conclusions}

In 27,680 UK Biobank participants (56.2\% female), HVR demonstrated
substantial head-size independence (r\textsubscript{ICV} = -0.25; 58\%
reduction vs.~raw HC), a consistent female advantage replicated in
ICV-matched samples (d = 0.18), and comparable brain-cognition
associations to hippocampal volume (HVR → \emph{g}: \(\beta\) = 0.043
vs.~HC → \emph{g}: \(\beta\) = 0.04, overlapping CIs). In contrast,
hippocampal sex differences reversed direction across adjustment methods
(d range: 1.5 SD), confirming their methodological determination. HVR
maintained predictive validity in both sexes, while residualized HC lost
significance in males (p = 0.227), and supported more complex SEM
specifications that raw HC could not.

Males showed 1.66× steeper age-related HVR differences (p = 2e-61),
consistent with earlier ventricular expansion signatures in males
{[}Currà et al., 2019{]} and evidence that hippocampal trajectory slopes
predict dementia conversion {[}Rajagopal et al., 2024{]}. Sex-specific
normative centile curves are provided for research application and
potential clinical use, pending validation in clinical populations.
